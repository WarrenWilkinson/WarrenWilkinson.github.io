Variance

Okay, so it's σ^2 = (b-a)^2/12. Where does that come from?

Given

\begin{equation} f(x) = \frac{1}{x_{max} - x_{min}} \end{equation} \begin{equation} P_{ab} = \frac{(b -a)}{(x_{max} - x_{min})} \end{equation} \begin{equation} E(x) = \frac{b + a}{2} \end{equation}

Also, I've been told:

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = E(x^2) - E(x)^2 \end{equation}

But I don't know where that comes from.

Variance from difference of Expectations

So the variance is the sum of square distances from the mean.

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int_x (x - \mu)^2 P(x) \end{equation}

a x (expand, cursor on the ^)

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int_x x^2 P(x) - 2 \int_x \mu x P(x) + \int_x \mu^2 P(x) \end{equation}

That expanded more than I'd expected, as it also carried the integral sign and the P(x) too. Which was gonna be my next steps anyway. The last term,

\begin{equation} \int_x \mu^2 P(x) \end{equation}

Is equal to just μ^2, because the area under the Probability density function is equal to 1.

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int_x x^2 P(x) - 2 \int_x \mu x P(x) + \mu^2 \end{equation}

The second term,

\begin{equation} \int_x \mu x P(x) \end{equation}

Is going to be mu times the value of x P(x) – which is the weighted probability, which is just the expectation μ again.

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int_x x^2 P(x) - 2 \mu^2 + \mu^2 \end{equation}

And that simplifies too

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int_x x^2 P(x) - \mu^2 \end{equation}

So I guess what we are saying is that

\begin{equation} E(x^2) = \int_x x^2 P(x) \end{equation}

Which is a strange formula for me, because P(x) has stayed P(x) and didn't become P(x^2). I guess that's why, in wikipedia they wrote it as

\begin{equation} P(X = x) \end{equation}

Which I'll do as well from now on, to show that this is what we're talking about. Interesting idea anyway. Glad to know it.

Variance of uniform distribution

Now, using

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = E(x^2) - E(x)^2 \end{equation} \begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int x^2 P(X = x) - \frac{a+b}{2}^2 \end{equation} \begin{equation} \sigma^{2} = \int x^{2} P\left( X = x \right) - \frac{(a + b)^{2}}{4} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \sigma^{2} = \int x^{2} P\left( X = x \right) - \frac{b^{2} + 2 a b + a^{2}}{4} \end{equation}

Since P(x = X) is 1/(b - a)

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \int \frac{x^2}{b-a} - \frac{b^2 + 2 a b + a^2}{4} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \Bigg [_{a}^{b}\frac{x^3}{3(b - a)} - \frac{b^2 + 2 a b + a^2}{4} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \frac{b^3 - a^3}{3(b - a)} - \frac{b^2 + 2 a b + a^2}{4} \end{equation}

j s to select it the first term, a n to normalize ??

\begin{equation} \sigma^{2} = \frac{b^{2} + a b + a^{2}}{3} - \frac{b^{2} + 2 a b + a^{2}}{4} \end{equation}

then a f (calc factor)

\begin{equation} 12 \sigma^2 = (b - a)^2 \end{equation}

Then j / 12 (divide both sides by twelve)

\begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \frac{(b - a)^2}{12} \end{equation}

And there we have it. So the math is right, I'm just not entirely sure how calc did it. It's nice that it did though.
